1. Project overview

What this project asks

This research project investigates how ethical discourse in artificial intelligence is framed in scholarly literature. Rather than treating AI ethics as a single category, the project asks what assumptions papers make about where ethics resides: in the system itself, in the people who design it, in the data it reflects, or in the context of its use.

2. Analytical framework

The two-axis framework

Papers were evaluated along two intersecting dimensions. The first asks whether AI is treated primarily as a mirror of human behavior, data, bias, or institutions, or as a self-portrait of human design choices, intentions, and constructed values. The second asks whether ethics is framed as a checklist problem of compliance and control, or as a situated problem shaped by context, relationships, meaning-making, and lived use.

3. Method

Research method

The project used a systematic literature review approach combined with qualitative coding. The review identified relevant papers, narrowed them using inclusion criteria, and then evaluated accepted papers through an iterative coding process.

  • Papers had to engage meaningfully with human-technology interaction, AI, or ethical framing.
  • Papers were screened for relevance rather than accepted solely because they mentioned ethics.
  • Both explicit and implicit ethical language were considered during coding.
  • Framework categories were refined as recurring patterns became visible across papers.
4. Why coding matters

From reading to classification

Coding transformed close reading into a repeatable method. Instead of simply summarizing papers, the process asked: what kind of ethical problem is being described, where is responsibility located, and what language signals the paper's assumptions about AI?

5. Coding process

How each paper was evaluated

Each paper moved through the same general workflow. The goal was not to force a paper into a quadrant too early, but to read for patterns, compare evidence, and justify placement using language from the paper itself.

1

Initial read

Read the title, abstract, keywords, and conclusion to identify the paper's main claim, domain, and ethical stance.

2

Locate ethical language

Mark phrases related to bias, responsibility, agency, governance, human values, meaning, interaction, fairness, trust, safety, or social context.

3

Determine the AI lens

Ask whether the paper treats AI primarily as a reflection of human data and society (mirror) or as a product of human design choices and intentions (self-portrait).

4

Determine the ethics lens

Ask whether the paper frames ethics as compliance, control, and requirements (checklist) or as relational, contextual, and evolving in practice (situated).

5

Assign provisional placement

Place the paper in the quadrant that best matches its dominant framing, while noting overlap, tension, or ambiguity.

6

Write justification notes

Record why the paper was placed there, including representative phrases, recurring themes, and any reasons the paper could plausibly sit near a boundary rather than at the center of one quadrant.

6. Example walkthrough

How one paper would be coded

The example below shows the logic of the coding process. Replace this sample paper description with one from your own review, and the structure will still work. Think of this as the research equivalent of showing your work, not just your answer.

Worked paper example

Paper: What Is Meaningful Human-Computer Interaction? Understanding Freedom, Responsibility, and Noos in HCI Based on Viktor Frankl’s Existential Philosophy

Core claim: HCI should understand users as meaning-seeking subjects and design with attention to freedom, responsibility, and noos.

Why it matters here: This paper does not reduce ethics to fairness metrics or compliance requirements. It explicitly links design intent, embedded values, interaction, and situated meaning-making.

Step-by-step interpretation

  • What is the paper mostly concerned with?
    It asks what meaningful HCI is and how freedom, responsibility, and noos can be understood in relation to technology design and use.
  • What language signals the mirror dimension?
    The paper emphasizes users as meaning-seeking subjects, highlights lived interaction, and discusses meaning as something that emerges through engagement, context, and use.
  • What language signals the self-portrait dimension?
    The paper repeatedly points to designers' intentions, embedded notions of users, inscribed values, and the responsibility of designers in shaping technological artifacts.
  • What language signals situated ethics?
    The paper argues that the existentials are interwoven, dynamic, and dependent on social and situational context. It rejects the idea that meaningfulness can be reduced to static traits or simple technical criteria.
  • Does it fit checklist ethics?
    Only weakly. The paper mentions responsible AI and ethical requirements as part of the broader discourse, but its main contribution is not a compliance framework. Its center of gravity is clearly contextual and interpretive.
  • What quadrant does this suggest?
    This is best coded as a hybrid paper: high mirror, high self-portrait, low checklist, and high situated ethics.

Provisional coding result

Mirror High — the paper centers users as meaning-seeking beings and emphasizes interaction, interpretation, and lived experience.
Self-Portrait High — the paper explicitly addresses designer intent, embedded values, notions of the user, and the responsibility built into design.
Checklist Ethics Low — compliance language appears at the edges, but the paper does not frame ethics primarily as standards, metrics, or procedural control.
Situated Ethics High — ethics is framed as contextual, relational, interwoven, and dependent on interaction, use, and social conditions.
Overall placement Hybrid / interdependent, leaning strongly toward the situated side of the framework.

What gets written into the notes

This paper is a strong theoretical anchor for the framework because it makes both sides of the mirror/self-portrait axis visible at once. On the one hand, it treats users as meaning-seeking subjects whose experience emerges through interaction, context, and use. On the other hand, it explicitly argues that technologies embed designer intentions, values, and assumptions about the user. Ethically, the paper is not centered on a checklist model. Instead, it frames meaningful HCI as dynamic, situated, and shaped by the interwovenness of freedom, responsibility, and noos across outcome, embeddedness, interaction, and usage. For coding purposes, it should be treated as a gold-standard example of a hybrid paper with strong situated emphasis.

7. Coding rubric

Questions used while coding

Mirror or Self-Portrait?

  • Does the paper describe AI as reflecting human data, institutions, or social behavior?
  • Does it instead emphasize human design choices, intentions, or embedded values?
  • Is the system treated as an outcome of history, or as an artifact of deliberate construction?

Checklist or Situated?

  • Is ethics framed as compliance, policy, principles, benchmarks, or technical safeguards?
  • Does the paper emphasize context, use, relationships, interpretation, or meaning-making in practice?
  • Are ethical problems treated as stable requirements or as evolving conditions of lived interaction?

Evidence in the paper

  • What repeated terms or metaphors appear across abstract, method, and discussion?
  • What counts as harm, responsibility, or success in the authors' framing?
  • What is left out that might reveal the paper's assumptions?

Ambiguity handling

  • Does the paper sit clearly in one quadrant or along a boundary?
  • Which dimension is strongest, and which is more mixed?
  • What note should be recorded to explain uncertainty for future review?
8. Outputs and future use

What this process produces

The immediate output of this method is a structured map of papers across the framework. A second output is a labeled dataset that records paper metadata, coded placement, and justification notes. That dataset creates a foundation for future phases, including comparison across subfields, trend analysis, and possible supervised classification.
